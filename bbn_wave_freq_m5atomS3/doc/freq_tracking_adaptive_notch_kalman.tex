\begin{abstract}
This note gives a clean and practical description of the KalmANF frequency
tracker as implemented in C\texttt{++}, following the single-parameter
adaptive notch formulation of Ali and van Waterschoot~\cite{AliWaterschoot2023}.
The method tracks the dominant sinusoidal component of a real-valued signal by
adapting the coefficient of a second-order resonator with a scalar Kalman
update.

The document describes what the code actually computes at each sample, how the
main parameters affect behavior, and how the additional engineering outputs
such as phase, confidence, and lock are formed. The focus is on the concrete
implementation rather than on abstract derivations.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
frequency estimation, adaptive notch filter, Kalman filter, sinusoidal
tracking, embedded signal processing
\end{IEEEkeywords}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

The KalmANF tracker estimates the dominant frequency of a scalar oscillatory
signal sample by sample. It does this by adapting a single parameter,
\begin{equation}
  a = 2\cos(\hat\omega_d),
\end{equation}
where $\hat\omega_d$ is the estimated digital radian frequency in
rad/sample. Once $a$ is known, the physical frequency in hertz follows
directly.

The implementation combines two simple ideas:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item a second-order resonator that concentrates energy around the tracked
        frequency, and
  \item a scalar Kalman update that adjusts the resonator coefficient.
\end{enumerate}

This structure is lightweight, easy to tune, and suitable for embedded
streaming applications.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Signal Model}

Let $y[n]$ denote the measured scalar input at sample $n$, with physical
sample period $\Delta t$. In the intended operating regime, $y[n]$ is
dominated by a single slowly varying sinusoidal component,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:signal}
  y(t) = A(t)\sin\!\bigl(\omega(t)t + \phi(t)\bigr) + \nu(t),
\end{equation}
where $A(t)$ is the amplitude, $\omega(t)$ is the angular frequency,
$\phi(t)$ is the phase, and $\nu(t)$ is additive disturbance or noise.

The tracker assumes one dominant tone. If several strong tones are present,
the adaptive coefficient usually settles near whichever tone produces the
largest effective resonator response.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Adaptive Resonator Structure}\label{sec:resonator}

\subsection{Transfer Function}

The core resonator is described by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tf}
  H(z^{-1};a)=
  \frac{1-a z^{-1}+z^{-2}}
       {1-\rho a z^{-1}+\rho^2 z^{-2}},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item $a = 2\cos(\hat\omega_d)$ is the adaptive coefficient,
  \item $\rho \in (0,1)$ is the pole radius.
\end{itemize}

The zeros lie on the unit circle at the tracked digital frequency, while the
poles lie slightly inside the unit circle at the same angle. In practical
terms, this gives a narrow resonance near the tracked frequency and helps the
filter concentrate on the dominant sinusoidal component.

\subsection{Resonator State Recursion}

The implementation keeps two past resonator states and computes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:res_state}
  s[n] = y[n] + \rho\,a\,s[n-1] - \rho^2 s[n-2].
\end{equation}

In the code, these stored states are
\begin{align}
  s[n-1] &\leftrightarrow \texttt{s\_prev1},\\
  s[n-2] &\leftrightarrow \texttt{s\_prev2}.
\end{align}

After each update, the history is shifted:
\begin{align}
  s[n-2] &\leftarrow s[n-1],\\
  s[n-1] &\leftarrow s[n].
\end{align}

\subsection{Frequency Recovery from $a$}

Given $a\in(-2,2)$, the digital radian frequency is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:omega_d}
  \hat\omega_d = \arccos\!\left(\frac{a}{2}\right),
\end{equation}
and the physical frequency in hertz is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:f_hz}
  \hat f = \frac{\hat\omega_d}{2\pi\,\Delta t}.
\end{equation}

This mapping is exactly what the code uses:
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{omega\_hat = acos(a/2)}, \qquad
  \texttt{f\_est\_hz = omega\_hat / (2\pi dt)}.
\end{equation}

To keep \eqref{eq:omega_d} real-valued, the code clamps $a$ to
$(-1.99999,1.99999)$ after each update.

\subsection{Interpretation of $a$}

The coefficient $a$ directly encodes digital frequency:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item $a \approx 2$ corresponds to very low frequency, near DC,
  \item $a \approx 0$ corresponds to about one quarter of the sample rate,
  \item $a \approx -2$ corresponds to frequency near Nyquist.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pole Radius and Selectivity}

The pole radius $\rho$ controls the resonator sharpness.
Larger $\rho$ gives narrower bandwidth, stronger selectivity, and longer
memory. Smaller $\rho$ gives broader bandwidth, faster settling, and greater
robustness to frequency drift and noise.

A commonly used approximation for the resonator bandwidth is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BW} \approx \frac{1-\rho}{\pi\,\Delta t}.
\end{equation}

The implementation default is
\begin{equation}
  \rho = 0.995.
\end{equation}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Scalar Kalman Adaptation}\label{sec:kalman}

\subsection{Tracked Parameter and Covariance}

The Kalman state is the scalar coefficient $a$. The code also maintains the
scalar covariance $p$, stored as \texttt{p\_cov}. The tuning parameters are:
\begin{align}
  q &:\ \text{process noise variance on } a,\\
  r &:\ \text{measurement noise variance on the innovation},\\
  p &:\ \text{current covariance on } a.
\end{align}

Their practical roles are straightforward:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item larger $q$ makes the tracker adapt faster but with noisier output,
  \item larger $r$ makes updates more conservative and smoother,
  \item larger $p$ gives larger Kalman gain and more aggressive correction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Innovation Used by the Code}

After computing the resonator output $s[n]$, the implementation forms the
innovation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:innovation}
  e[n] = s[n] - a[n-1]\,s[n-1] + s[n-2].
\end{equation}

This is the exact expression used in the header:
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{e = s - s\_prev1 * a + s\_prev2}.
\end{equation}

The innovation is the scalar error signal used to correct the adaptive
coefficient.

\subsection{Prediction Step}

Before the measurement update, the covariance is propagated as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pred}
  p_{n|n-1} = p_{n-1} + q.
\end{equation}

In code this is:
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{p\_cov += q}.
\end{equation}

The value is then floored at $10^{-12}$ to avoid degeneracy.

\subsection{Kalman Gain}

The Kalman gain used in the implementation is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gain}
  K[n] = \frac{s[n-1]}{s[n-1]^2 + r/p_{n|n-1}}.
\end{equation}

This is computed directly from the current stored state and covariance.
The implementation also adds a tiny numerical guard to the denominator.

It is often convenient to view this as a scalar EKF-style update with
effective observation factor
\begin{equation}
  H[n] = s[n-1],
\end{equation}
but the code does not explicitly form a Jacobian matrix; it just evaluates
\eqref{eq:gain} directly.

\subsection{Parameter Update}

The adaptive coefficient is updated by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a_update}
  a[n] = a[n-1] + K[n]\,e[n].
\end{equation}

Afterward the code clamps $a[n]$ to the valid $\arccos$ interval:
\begin{equation}
  a[n] \in (-1.99999,1.99999).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Covariance Update}

The covariance update is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p_update}
  p_n = \bigl(1 - K[n]\,s[n-1]\bigr)\,p_{n|n-1}.
\end{equation}

This is then floored at $10^{-12}$.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Per-Sample Update Sequence}\label{sec:update}

For each valid input sample $(y[n],\Delta t)$, the implementation performs the
following operations in order.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item Check that $y[n]$ is finite and $\Delta t > 0$. If not, return the
        previous frequency estimate unchanged.
  \item Compute the new resonator state
        \[
          s[n] = y[n] + \rho a s[n-1] - \rho^2 s[n-2].
        \]
  \item Predict the covariance:
        \[
          p \leftarrow p + q.
        \]
  \item Compute the Kalman gain
        \[
          K = \frac{s[n-1]}{s[n-1]^2 + r/p}.
        \]
  \item Form the innovation
        \[
          e = s[n] - a s[n-1] + s[n-2].
        \]
  \item Update the coefficient
        \[
          a \leftarrow a + K e,
        \]
        and clamp it to $(-1.99999,1.99999)$.
  \item Update the covariance
        \[
          p \leftarrow (1-Ks[n-1])p,
        \]
        and floor it at $10^{-12}$.
  \item Compute the frequency estimate
        \[
          \hat f = \frac{\arccos(a/2)}{2\pi\Delta t}.
        \]
  \item Store the new resonator state:
        \[
          s[n-2] \leftarrow s[n-1],\qquad
          s[n-1] \leftarrow s[n].
        \]
  \item Update the confidence and lock diagnostics.
\end{enumerate}

This order matches the code.

\section{Initialization}\label{sec:init}

\subsection{Default Initialization}

The default initializer sets
\begin{align}
  \rho &= 0.995,\\
  q &= 10^{-5},\\
  r &= 10^3,\\
  p_0 &= 1,\\
  a_0 &= 1.9999,
\end{align}
with resonator history
\begin{equation}
  s[-1]=0,\qquad s[-2]=0.
\end{equation}

A noteworthy implementation detail is that the ordinary \texttt{init()}
function does \emph{not} compute an initial frequency from $a_0$. It simply
sets
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{f\_est\_hz = 0}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Frequency-Seeded Initialization}

The convenience method \texttt{initFromFreqGuess()} seeds the coefficient from
an initial frequency guess:
\begin{equation}
  a_0 = 2\cos(2\pi f_{\rm guess}\Delta t).
\end{equation}

It then initializes the reported frequency to
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{f\_est\_hz = } \max(f_{\rm guess},0),
\end{equation}
and also initializes the smoothed frequency used by the confidence logic.

This is the most practical way to start the tracker when a reasonable
frequency range is already known.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Tuning Parameters}\label{sec:tuning}

\subsection{Pole Radius $\rho$}

The parameter $\rho$ controls the resonator memory and selectivity.
A value close to one is narrowband and selective. A smaller value is broader
and more forgiving.

\subsection{Process Noise $q$}

The process noise controls how quickly the tracker forgets the current value
of $a$. Larger $q$ means quicker adaptation to frequency changes, but more
jitter in the estimate. Smaller $q$ means smoother tracking, but slower
response.

\subsection{Measurement Noise $r$}

The measurement noise controls how much the innovation is trusted.
Larger $r$ reduces the Kalman gain and produces more conservative updates.
Smaller $r$ increases the gain and makes the tracker respond more aggressively.

\subsection{Covariance $p$}

The covariance $p$ is internal state rather than a user-facing signal, but it
has an intuitive interpretation: it represents uncertainty in the current
coefficient estimate. Large $p$ produces large gain; small $p$ produces small
gain.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Phase Output}\label{sec:phase}

The resonator exposes a phase-like diagnostic through
\begin{equation}
  \hat\phi[n] = \operatorname{atan2}(s[n],\,s[n-1]).
\end{equation}

This follows from the implementation:
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{get\_phase() = atan2(s\_prev1, s\_prev2)}.
\end{equation}

Because the state history is shifted before phase is queried, this
corresponds to the current stored pair $(s[n],s[n-1])$.

This quantity is useful as a smooth tracking diagnostic, but it should be
understood as a practical phase-like output rather than a formally exact
analytic-signal phase.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Sensitivity to DC Offset}\label{sec:dc}

The tracker does not inherently reject DC offsets or very slow bias drift.
A constant offset in the input can accumulate in the resonator states and
bias the innovation signal, which in turn can bias the adaptive coefficient
and therefore the frequency estimate.

For practical sensor applications, especially those involving accelerometer
signals or tilt leakage, a slow mean-removal or high-pass stage before the
tracker is recommended.

Common mitigation choices are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item subtract a running mean,
  \item apply a gentle high-pass filter,
  \item estimate and remove DC separately upstream.
\end{itemize}

\section{Confidence and Lock Outputs}\label{sec:lock}

The underlying KalmANF tracker estimates frequency, but it does not natively
provide a lock detector. The implementation therefore adds a synthesized
confidence and lock layer.

\subsection{Signal RMS}

After the resonator state has been updated, the code forms the instantaneous
signal-level proxy
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm sig,inst}[n] = y[n]^2 + 0.5\,s[n]^2.
\end{equation}

This uses the \emph{current} stored resonator state $s[n]$, because the
history shift is performed before the confidence update.

A one-pole smoother is then applied:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha(\Delta t,\tau) = \frac{\Delta t}{\tau+\Delta t},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm sig} \leftarrow P_{\rm sig}
  + \alpha(\Delta t,\tau_{\rm rms})
    \bigl(P_{\rm sig,inst}[n]-P_{\rm sig}\bigr),
\end{equation}
and the reported RMS-like signal level is
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\rm sig} = \sqrt{\max(P_{\rm sig},0)}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Innovation RMS}

The implementation also smooths the squared innovation:
\begin{equation}
  P_e \leftarrow P_e + \alpha(\Delta t,\tau_e)\bigl(e[n]^2 - P_e\bigr),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_e = \sqrt{\max(P_e,0)}.
\end{equation}

Smaller innovation relative to signal level indicates that the resonator is
tracking the dominant sinusoidal component more consistently.

\subsection{Smoothed Frequency}

A separate one-pole smoother tracks the frequency estimate:
\begin{equation}
  \bar f \leftarrow \bar f
  + \alpha(\Delta t,\tau_f)\bigl(\hat f - \bar f\bigr).
\end{equation}

This is used only for stability scoring and does not affect the main tracker
state.

\subsection{Frequency Validity Window}

The frequency estimate is considered valid when
\begin{equation}
  f_{\min} \le \hat f \le f_{\max},
\end{equation}
with default bounds
\begin{equation}
  f_{\min} = \SI{0.03}{Hz}, \qquad
  f_{\max} = \SI{2.0}{Hz}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Confidence Score}

When the frequency is inside the valid range, the code computes three
subscores.

First, an amplitude score:
\begin{equation}
  r_{\rm amp} =
  \operatorname{clamp}_{[0,1]}
  \left(
    \frac{\sigma_{\rm sig}}
         {\max(\sigma_{\rm rms,min},10^{-9})}
  \right).
\end{equation}

Second, a relative-error score:
\begin{equation}
  r_{\rm err} =
  1-\min\!\left(
    1,\,
    \frac{\sigma_e / \max(\sigma_{\rm sig},10^{-9})}
         {\gamma}
  \right),
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ is the soft relative-error threshold
(\texttt{rel\_err\_lock\_soft}, default $1.25$).

Third, a frequency-stability score:
\begin{equation}
  d = \max\!\left(0.03,\ 0.25\max(\bar f,f_{\min})\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  r_{\rm stab} =
  1-\min\!\left(1,\frac{|\hat f-\bar f|}{d}\right).
\end{equation}

The confidence target is then
\begin{equation}
  c^\star =
  r_{\rm amp}\bigl(0.20 + 0.45\,r_{\rm err} + 0.35\,r_{\rm stab}\bigr).
\end{equation}

If the frequency is outside the valid window, the confidence target is set
to zero.

Finally, the reported confidence is another one-pole smoothed version of this
target:
\begin{equation}
  c \leftarrow c + \alpha(\Delta t,\tau_c)(c^\star-c),
\end{equation}
followed by clamping to $[0,1]$.

\subsection{Lock Flag}

The lock flag is set when all of the following are true:
\begin{align}
  &\hat f \in [f_{\min},f_{\max}],\\
  &\sigma_{\rm sig} \ge \sigma_{\rm rms,min},\\
  &c \ge c_{\rm lock}.
\end{align}

So lock is simply a practical decision based on plausible frequency, adequate
signal level, and sustained confidence.

\section{Numerical Safeguards}\label{sec:numerical}

The implementation includes several small but important numerical protections:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item $p$ is floored at $10^{-12}$ after prediction and update,
  \item the Kalman-gain denominator includes a tiny additive guard,
  \item $a$ is clamped to the valid $\arccos$ domain,
  \item non-finite input or invalid timestep causes the update to be skipped.
\end{itemize}

These are practical engineering safeguards for floating-point robustness.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Summary}

The implemented KalmANF tracker is a clean single-parameter frequency tracker
with the following structure:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item a second-order adaptive resonator,
  \item a scalar Kalman update on the coefficient $a$,
  \item direct conversion from $a$ to frequency through $\arccos(a/2)$,
  \item additional practical diagnostics for phase, confidence, and lock.
\end{itemize}

The code is compact, easy to interpret, and well suited to embedded tracking
of a dominant narrowband oscillatory signal.
